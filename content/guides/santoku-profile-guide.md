## Why the shape works

Santoku roughly translates to three virtues, commonly interpreted around meat, fish and vegetables. In practice, it is a compact all-purpose knife with a lower tip, moderate height and a cutting edge that prefers short push cuts over dramatic rocking.

The size is the appeal. A 165mm or 180mm santoku fits smaller boards, shared kitchens and cooks who want control more than reach.

## Where it is weaker

The lower tip is not as nimble for trimming and deep point work as a gyuto or chef knife. The profile also has less belly, so a santoku can feel awkward if your default motion is a big rocking cut.

Grantons or hollow dimples can help with sticking in some foods, but they do not override grind quality. A thick santoku still wedges, even if the blade has dimples.

## Who should buy it

Buy a santoku if you cook in a small space, prep a lot of vegetables and want a stable blade that does not feel long. Skip it if you already own a gyuto you like and rarely feel cramped on the board.

## Takeaways

- Good for small boards and controlled push cuts.
- Not ideal for heavy rocking or precise tip trimming.
- A thin grind matters more than decorative dimples.
