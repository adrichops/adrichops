## Use a gentle board

Wood, soft synthetic and rubber-style boards are kinder to edges than glass, stone or very hard boards. The board should have a little give. If the knife hits the surface and the edge loses the argument, your sharpening schedule gets unpleasant.

For thin Japanese knives, board choice matters even more. A hard board plus twisting technique can produce chips that no amount of romantic steel talk will fix.

## Size matters more than novelty

A board should be large enough that food does not pile under the handle. Small boards create cramped technique, and cramped technique leads to tip twisting, awkward angles and general nonsense.

If the board slides, put a damp towel or non-slip mat under it. Stable board, stable cuts, fewer emergency sound effects.

## Storage protects the apex

A knife guard, roll, block or tray prevents edge collisions. If the knife is stored loose with peelers, spoons and the mysterious drawer object nobody can identify, the edge is getting micro-damaged between uses.

For carbon knives, storage also needs dryness and airflow. Do not trap moisture in a saya or guard after washing. Dry first, then store.

## Travel and shared kitchens

Use blade guards when moving knives. If you cook in shared spaces, label the knives that need special care or keep them separate. Many chipped tips are not accidents; they are communication failures with handles.

For a shared kitchen, a forgiving stainless workhorse may be smarter than a fragile thin gyuto. This is not defeat. This is strategy.

## Takeaways

- Soft boards extend edge life.
- Safe storage prevents invisible damage.
- Shared kitchens need forgiving knives or very clear rules.
